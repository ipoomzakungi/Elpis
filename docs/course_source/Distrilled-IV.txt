2. Core Philosophy: Measurement, Not Prediction
The source framework is built on the idea that traders should stop guessing price direction and instead measure where the market has priced risk incorrectly. Part 1 says the institutional/quant approach is not “where will price go?” but “is volatility mispriced?” It uses normalization and cross-sectional comparison to identify outliers.
This matters because a trading signal should not begin with a directional opinion. It should begin with a market state:
Is IV expensive or cheap?
Is price inside or outside expected move?
Where is OI concentrated?
Where is Gamma concentrated?
Is current flow activating those levels?
Is the market in mean-reversion or momentum regime?
This is why the Mtraders visual material repeatedly frames the method as looking through the Volatility Lens: instead of seeing only price candles, you measure volatility, commitment, and hidden structure under price.

3. OI: What It Is
Open Interest, or OI, is the number of contracts that remain open after clearing/settlement. It is not the same as volume.
The tool explanation says Put OI and Call OI are the total open interest of all put and call contracts in the selected series, calculated by summing OI across every strike. It is useful for seeing which side has more outstanding position weight, and it can be used to calculate Put/Call Ratio. But the same source warns that intraday volume often reflects the real action during the day better than OI, because OI is accumulated/settled position data.
The IV lesson also makes the timing difference clear: OI is the settled outstanding contract quantity, often cleared from the previous day, while intraday data is today’s live trading activity.
So:
OI = committed position map
Volume = current activity
OI Change = change in committed position
Intraday Volume/OI = whether today’s activity is large relative to existing commitment
3.1 How OI Changes
OI increases when:
New buyer opens + new seller opens
OI decreases when:
Existing long closes + existing short closes
OI stays the same when:
One trader opens while another trader closes
Therefore, raw OI cannot tell you:
Who is long?
Who is short?
Was the trade opening?
Was the trade closing?
Was the market maker forced to hedge?
That is why OI must be combined with:
Volume
OI change
Bid/ask trade direction
Open/close data
Delta
Gamma
IV change
Price reaction

4. The Most Important OI Rule
The most important rule is:
OI is real, but OI is delayed and sign-ambiguous.
The source says “OI is real,” but it also distinguishes OI from intraday activity. OI is settled outstanding position; intraday volume is the live “changing of hands.” The IV lesson’s source guide says intraday volume is treated as the “hero” indicator for daily movement because it reflects real-time institutional action, while OI reflects settled positions from the prior clearing cycle.
For automation:
Use OI to build the battlefield.
Use intraday volume to see where the battle is active.
Use price to confirm who is winning.
Do not automate like this:
IF Put OI high THEN buy
IF Call OI high THEN sell
Use this instead:
IF price approaches high-OI strike
AND the strike is also high gamma
AND IV is compressing
AND intraday flow does not break the level
THEN mean-reversion signal is allowed

IF price breaks high-OI strike
AND volume/OI surges
AND IV expands
AND gamma regime is negative or flow implies short-gamma hedging
THEN momentum/squeeze signal is allowed

7. Delta Ranges and SD Ranges
This was under-covered in v1.
Delta Ranges are not just decorative chart bands. They are market-implied distance zones.
From the Vol2Vol source:
25Δ zone = closer OTM / first meaningful wing
15Δ zone = further OTM / larger expected move
5Δ zone = deep OTM / tail risk zone
The tool shows examples where:
25Δ distance ≈ 54.5 points
15Δ distance ≈ 108–109.9 points
5Δ distance ≈ 160.9–166 points
These are used as quant measurements for Delta-Gamma exposure and tail-risk distance.
For automation, use both:
Distance in price points
Distance in SD
Distance in delta bucket
Example classification:
ATM / 50Δ = center
25Δ = first pressure zone
15Δ = high-stress zone
5Δ = tail zone
A price move from ATM toward 25Δ is normal. A move toward 15Δ is more serious. A move toward 5Δ means the market is entering tail-risk territory, especially if IV expands and volume follows.

8. IV and Expected Move
IV is the market’s price of future movement. It is not a directional forecast.
The SD source says Daily Expected Move compresses annualized IV into a one-day estimate, while exact time to expiration should be used for contracts with less than one day remaining, such as 0DTE. It also states that 1 SD covers approximately 68.2% probability.
8.1 Expected Move Formula
Use annualized IV as decimal:
Expected_Move = Underlying_Price × IV × sqrt(Time_To_Expiration_In_Years)
For a one-day approximation:
Daily_1SD_% ≈ Annualized_IV / 16
Daily_1SD_Points = Price × Daily_1SD_%
For 0DTE exact timing:
T = Minutes_To_Expiration / Minutes_Per_Trading_Year
Expected_Move_Remaining = Current_Price × Current_IV × sqrt(T)
8.2 SD Bands
Upper_1SD = Anchor_Price + 1 × Expected_Move
Lower_1SD = Anchor_Price - 1 × Expected_Move

Upper_2SD = Anchor_Price + 2 × Expected_Move
Lower_2SD = Anchor_Price - 2 × Expected_Move

Upper_3SD = Anchor_Price + 3 × Expected_Move
Lower_3SD = Anchor_Price - 3 × Expected_Move
Approximate normal distribution interpretation:
±1 SD ≈ 68.2%
±2 SD ≈ 95%
±3 SD ≈ 99.7%
But this is not a guarantee. Part 4 warns that real markets have fat tails, gap risk, jumps, weekend gaps, geopolitical events, and Black Swan behavior. The model is a map, not the territory.

9. CME Settlement Lock: Critical for Automation
The SD formula source says the map should be built around a locked time. It states that around 00:30, or 01:30 during DST, CME performs mark-to-market accounting. At that point, the system pulls fixed constants and draws ±1SD, ±2SD, and ±3SD bands on the chart. These lines should remain fixed and not move with real-time price. The constants include prior settlement price and Vol Settle/ATM IV.
This is very important.
Your automation should separate:
Static Daily Map:
    prior settlement
    vol settlement
    fixed SD bands
    prior OI

Dynamic Intraday Map:
    current price
    current IV
    intraday volume
    live Greeks
    live flow
Do not keep moving the original daily SD map every tick. That destroys the meaning of the risk boundary.

10. Why 1SD, 2SD, and 3SD Matter for Trade Location
The IV instructional source explains the practical risk logic: as price moves farther toward the IV boundary, the remaining distance to the boundary shrinks, so the capital required to survive adverse movement becomes smaller. The source frames this as a risk-management advantage for mean reversion, not a guarantee that price cannot continue.
The distilled rule:
Inside ±1SD:
    normal zone
    less edge for mean reversion
    need flow confirmation

Near ±2SD:
    pressure zone
    mean reversion becomes interesting if positive gamma / IV compression / failed breakout

Near ±3SD:
    tail zone
    avoid blind fade
    trade only with strong reversal confirmation or strong squeeze confirmation
Important:
1SD is not automatically a trade.
2SD is not automatically a reversal.
3SD is not automatically impossible.
SD gives risk boundary. It does not replace flow confirmation.

11. Delta: The Correct Interpretation
Part 2 explains that Delta should be understood as hedge ratio / equivalent exposure, not merely probability of finishing ITM. A 0.50 Delta option behaves like exposure to 50 shares per 100-share stock option contract.
For futures options, translate this into futures-equivalent exposure.
Futures_Equivalent_Delta = Contracts × Delta
For gold futures options:
Dollar_Delta ≈ Contracts × Delta × Contract_Multiplier × Futures_Price
Delta tells you:
How directional the option position is now.
How much futures/underlying may be needed to hedge.
Delta does not tell you how fast exposure changes. That is Gamma.

12. Gamma: The Engine of Forced Hedging
Gamma is the change in Delta when price moves.
Part 2 says Gamma is what makes Delta non-constant. It creates convexity. Long Gamma can create “buy low, sell high” rebalancing behavior when the trader delta-hedges.
For 0DTE, Gamma becomes especially important. The gamma hedging source says that as time to expiration shortens to one day or less, Theta rises sharply, and for ATM or near-the-money strikes Gamma increases because small underlying moves can create large Delta changes. OTM Gamma falls as the probability of moving ITM decreases.
So:
ATM + short DTE = maximum Gamma danger/opportunity
Far OTM + low probability = less Gamma until price approaches
For automation:
High OI is not enough.
High OI × high Gamma is important.
High OI × high Gamma × active flow is very important.

13. Convert OI Into Exposure
Raw OI must be transformed.
13.1 OI Notional
For equity index options:
OI_Notional = OI × Contract_Multiplier × Underlying_Price
For standard equity-style index options, multiplier is often 100.
For CME Gold options, the Mtraders gold analysis states that COMEX Gold Options use underlying Gold Futures, contract size is 100 troy ounces, and 1 point = $100 USD.
So for Gold:
Gold_OI_Notional = OI × 100 × GC_Futures_Price
13.2 OI Delta Exposure
OI_Delta_Exposure = OI × Delta × Contract_Multiplier × Price
For futures-equivalent contracts:
Futures_Equivalent = OI × Delta
13.3 OI Gamma Exposure
General:
OI_Gamma_Exposure = OI × Gamma × Contract_Multiplier × Price²
For strategy-level or leg-level features, the 0DTE Trading Rules paper defines flow and GEX-style features using volume, OI, Greeks, and spot. For example, flow gamma uses volume × gamma × 100 × spot², and OI gamma exposure uses OI × gamma × 100 × spot². It also separates liquidity, flow, and GEX features because they capture different economic channels: execution friction, current trading pressure, and concentration of convexity.
For Gold, replace 100 with the Gold contract multiplier:
Gold_OI_Gamma_Exposure = OI × Gamma × 100 × GC_Price²
The 100 here means 100 troy ounces, not 100 shares.

14. Signed vs Absolute Gamma
You need two versions.
Absolute Gamma
Abs_GEX = OI × abs(Gamma) × Multiplier × Price²
Use this to find:
Where convexity is concentrated
Where price may pin
Where hedging sensitivity is high
Where a break may matter
Signed Gamma
Signed_GEX = Position_Sign × OI × Gamma × Multiplier × Price²
The problem:
You usually do not know Position_Sign from raw OI.
So with only public OI, you can estimate:
Gamma concentration
Potential hedge sensitivity
Put wall / Call wall
Possible squeeze levels
But you cannot honestly claim:
Dealer is definitely long gamma
Dealer is definitely short gamma
unless you have open/close data, trade direction, or dealer inventory data.
The 0DTE Trading Rules paper explicitly notes that its flow-style and exposure-style proxies based on traded volume, OI, and Greeks are not a dealer-inventory reconstruction.
This is a crucial automation rule:
If dealer inventory data is unavailable:
    label the feature as GEX Proxy, not true dealer GEX.

The 0DTE Trading Rules paper gives useful normalized features:
BΓ = gOI,n / (abs(gOI,a) + 1)
RΓ = fΓ / (abs(gOI,a) + 1)
T  = h / (d + 1)
Meaning:
BΓ = normalized GEX balance
RΓ = gamma flow pressure scaled by local GEX base

This is important because raw flow and raw GEX are scale-dependent across strategies, strikes, premium sizes, and widths.
For your futures signal, translate this into:
GEX_Balance = Net_GEX_Proxy / (Abs_GEX + 1)

Flow_Pressure = Intraday_Gamma_Flow / (Abs_GEX + 1)

Signal meaning:
High Abs_GEX + low Flow_Pressure:
    level may pin / resist

High Abs_GEX + high Flow_Pressure:
    level is being attacked

High Flow_Pressure against wall + IV expansion:
    possible squeeze / breakout

High Liquidity_Tightness:

16. Positive Gamma vs Negative Gamma Regime
This is the heart of the trading signal.
Positive Dealer Gamma
When dealers/market makers are long gamma, hedging tends to lean against price movement.
Price rises -> dealer sells underlying/futures
Price falls -> dealer buys underlying/futures
Effect:
Mean reversion
Volatility dampening
Pinning
Failed breakouts
Range behavior
The liquidity-provider paper says that if market makers’ net gamma and net delta are positive, they should buy after index declines and sell after index increases, which dampens volatility. It finds that market makers’ 0DTE net gamma predicts lower volatility over the next 10 minutes and up to about one hour.
Negative Dealer Gamma
When dealers/market makers are short gamma, hedging tends to follow price movement.
Price rises -> dealer buys more
Price falls -> dealer sells more
Effect:
Momentum
Acceleration
Gamma squeeze
Volatility expansion
Trend persistence
The gamma hedging source explains that hedging short gamma requires trading in the direction of price movement, creating intraday momentum. It also says that balanced buy/sell flow can leave market makers with near-zero net gamma despite large notional volume.
Automation Translation
Positive Gamma Regime = fade extremes / expect pin / reduce breakout confidence

Negative Gamma Regime = follow breakouts / avoid blind fade / expect acceleration

Unknown Gamma Regime = reduce size / require stronger confirmation

17. Why Volume Alone Is Not Risk
A major academic point: huge 0DTE volume does not automatically mean huge hedging impact.
The gamma hedging source gives a clear example: if 100,000 contracts trade at a strike but 50,000 are customer buys and 50,000 are customer sells, market makers can be left with net zero gamma risk even though notional volume is huge.
So the model must distinguish:
Gross Volume
Net Flow
Order Imbalance
Dealer Inventory
Gamma Exposure
Bad signal:
Volume high -> market must move
Better signal:
Volume high
AND flow imbalance is directional
AND IV is expanding
AND price is breaking a gamma/OI level
AND dealer gamma regime supports momentum

18. What the Academic 0DTE Sources Really Say
The 0DTE literature is not one-sided. This matters because you should not hard-code one belief.
18.1 View A: 0DTE / Market-Maker Gamma Often Dampens Volatility
The Dim-Eraker-Vilkov paper finds that market makers’ 0DTE net gamma is on average positive and negatively related to future intraday volatility. It also finds that positive gamma strengthens intraday reversal, while negative gamma strengthens momentum.
The same paper reports that a one-standard-deviation increase in market makers’ 0DTE net gamma is associated with a decline in underlying log variance equal to 7.3% of its standard deviation, and that market makers are predominantly long gamma for most of the sample.
The Adams-Fontaine-Ornthanalai liquidity-provider paper finds that index volatility is lower by about 60–90 annualized basis points on days with 0DTE trading, and argues that market-maker hedging of expiring positions dampens volatility.
18.2 View B: 0DTE Trading Can Still Increase Volatility
Brogaard-Han-Won find that a one-standard-deviation increase in 0DTE options trading leads to a 9.10% increase relative to mean volatility. They argue that the volatility effect remains positive even after accounting for gamma hedging and is primarily driven by speculative retail investors.
Their internet appendix result says that 0DTE volume and order imbalance continue to exert positive pressure on volatility, while market-maker long gamma only partially offsets directional imbalance.
18.3 View C: Average Effect Can Be Dampening, But Negative Gamma Can Still Be Dangerous
The Amaya-Garcia-Ares-Pearson-Vasquez paper finds that OMM gamma is typically positive and often reduces volatility, but gamma can be negative. It estimates that the largest impact of OMM gamma was to increase annualized daily volatility by 3.3 percentage points and 30-minute annualized volatility by 6.4 percentage points.
It also notes that OMM gamma was negative at some point on at least 25% of days in its sample, and after Tuesday/Thursday expirations were introduced, daily minima were negative on at least half of trading days.
18.4 Final Research Conclusion
The correct automated model is not:
0DTE always dampens volatility
or:
0DTE always increases volatility
The correct model is:
Separate the channels:
    1. Dealer gamma inventory
    2. Directional order imbalance
    3. Retail/speculative flow
    4. IV expansion/compression
    5. Price location versus SD/OI walls
    6. Event/jump risk

19. Retail 0DTE Flow: Why It Matters
The retail 0DTE paper finds a strong retail preference for 0DTE options. It says that for most of 2022 and all of 2023, 0DTE options represented more than 75% of retail S&P 500 options volume, and the paper finds substantial retail losses despite price improvement mechanisms.
Another snippet from that paper says market-maker order imbalance in 0DTE options was consistently negative, meaning end consumers had net-long demand for 0DTE options and market makers absorbed that demand.
Signal meaning:
Retail / speculative flow can create noise volatility.
Dealer hedging can dampen or amplify.
The model must not assume all option flow is informed.
For automation, include:
small-lot volume share if available
single-leg vs multi-leg if available
same-day expiry share
volume/OI surge
put/call volume imbalance

20. Jump Risk and Event Risk
0DTE options are extremely sensitive to intraday jumps.
The Intraday Jumps paper says 0DTE options display extreme sensitivity to abrupt underlying price movements. It finds intraday jumps are frequent and impactful, with intensity clustering around the market open and close. It also finds that the implied premium for jump risk is nearly twice as large as the combined premia for diffusion and volatility risks.
The same paper gives an FOMC example where an out-of-the-money 0DTE SPX put rose dramatically after Powell’s comments; the option gained around 640% from the open and around 2400% from its intraday low.
Automation implication:
On FOMC / CPI / NFP / major news:
    SD mean-reversion rules become weaker
    jump filter must override normal signal
    size must be reduced or strategy must switch to event mode
Event mode logic:
Before event:
    avoid short naked gamma
    prefer defined-risk structures
    watch IV expansion

After event:
    if IV crush + price inside SD -> mean reversion allowed
    if IV expansion + price breaks OI wall -> momentum allowed

21. Model Risk: The Map Is Not the Territory
Part 4 warns against blindly trusting Black-Scholes or any Greek model. Markets have fat tails, gaps, jumps, weekends, geopolitical events, and discontinuous movement. A short-gamma trader who expects to rebalance continuously may be unable to rebalance through a gap.
This must be coded into risk rules.
Automation cannot say:
Price cannot exceed 3SD
It must say:
Price is statistically stretched, but tail events can break the map.
Risk controls:
No naked short gamma
No unlimited loss structures
No mean-reversion trade during high-impact event without confirmation
Hard stop
Daily loss limit
Data outage kill switch
Spread/liquidity filter

25. Strategy Evidence From 0DTE Trading Rules
The 0DTE Trading Rules paper is useful because it warns against broad unconditional rules.
It finds that a positive 0DTE variance risk premium exists, but same-day magnitude is small and difficult to monetize after realistic frictions. Strategy PNL is wide, state-dependent, and dominated by tail risk rather than stable carry. It also finds that conditional 10:00 ET rules can work better for selected strategies.
Its later results show that put ratio spreads, iron butterfly/condor structures, and diversified baskets can produce positive out-of-sample performance under selected conditional rules, while not all strategies work uniformly.
Automation conclusion:
Do not build an unconditional rule like:
    "Sell 0DTE every day"
    "Fade 2SD every time"
    "Buy every call-wall break"

Build conditional rules:
    only trade when regime + location + flow + liquidity agree

31. Trading Signal Logic
31.1 Mean Reversion Short
Use when price is stretched upward but not breaking into squeeze.
IF Price_Z >= +2.0
AND Price is near upper Call Wall or upper high Abs_GEX zone
AND Gamma_Regime is Positive or Gamma_Proxy supports pinning
AND IV is flat or falling
AND upside volume/OI is not surging
AND price fails to hold above wall
AND no major event window
THEN Short Futures
Exit:
Target 1 = +1SD
Target 2 = VWAP / ATM / settlement anchor
Stop = above +3SD or next call/gamma wall
31.2 Mean Reversion Long
IF Price_Z <= -2.0
AND Price is near lower Put Wall or lower high Abs_GEX zone
AND Gamma_Regime is Positive or Gamma_Proxy supports pinning
AND IV is flat or falling
AND downside volume/OI is not surging
AND price fails to hold below wall
AND no major event window
THEN Long Futures
Exit:
Target 1 = -1SD
Target 2 = VWAP / ATM / settlement anchor
Stop = below -3SD or next put/gamma wall
31.3 Momentum Long / Call-Side Gamma Squeeze
IF Price breaks above Call Wall
AND Price holds above broken strike
AND Call volume/OI surges
AND IV expands
AND Flow_Pressure is high
AND Gamma_Regime is Negative or unknown-but-flow strongly confirms
THEN Long Futures
Exit:
Target = next Call Wall / next Gamma Wall / upper SD band
Stop = back below broken Call Wall
31.4 Momentum Short / Put-Side Gamma Squeeze
IF Price breaks below Put Wall
AND Price holds below broken strike
AND Put volume/OI surges
AND IV expands
AND Flow_Pressure is high
AND Gamma_Regime is Negative or unknown-but-flow strongly confirms
THEN Short Futures
Exit:
Target = next Put Wall / lower SD band
Stop = back above broken Put Wall
31.5 Pinning / Gamma Magnet
IF Price is near high Abs_GEX strike
AND Gamma_Regime is Positive
AND IV is falling
AND price repeatedly rejects both sides
THEN expect chop/pin
Futures action:
Either no trade
or small mean-reversion scalps around the magnet
Options action:
Defined-risk short premium only
Never naked short gamma
31.6 No-Trade Conditions
No trade if:
    Price inside ±1SD and no strong flow
    IV flat and OI walls far away
    Spread too wide
    Liquidity too thin
    Data missing
    Major news within blackout window
    Signal conflict: positive gamma but IV exploding
    Signal conflict: negative gamma but breakout fails

32. Signal Scoring Model
Instead of binary rules only, use scores.
32.1 Mean Reversion Score
MR_Score =
    + Price beyond 2SD
    + Near high Abs_GEX wall
    + Positive gamma regime
    + IV falling
    + Volume fading
    + Failed breakout
    + Distance to IV boundary small
    - Negative gamma regime
    - IV expanding
    - Volume/OI surge in breakout direction
    - Event window
32.2 Momentum Score
MOM_Score =
    + Break of OI/Gamma wall
    + Hold above/below broken wall
    + Volume/OI surge
    + IV expansion
    + Negative gamma regime
    + Flow_Pressure high
    + Price outside 1SD/2SD with continuation
    - Positive gamma regime
    - Failed breakout
    - Liquidity poor
Decision:
IF MR_Score >= threshold AND MOM_Score low:
    mean reversion trade

IF MOM_Score >= threshold AND MR_Score low:
    momentum trade

IF both high:
    reduce size or wait

IF both low:
    no trade

34. Backtest Rules
Backtesting must avoid fake results.
34.1 No Lookahead
Use only OI that was available at that timestamp.
Do not use end-of-day OI during the same day unless the feed truly provides it live.
Use prior settlement and vol settlement as fixed daily constants.
Use only intraday volume up to current timestamp.

Backtest separately:

Negative gamma days
Unknown gamma days
Event days

High IV days
Low IV days
Trend days
Range days
Expiration days
Non-expiration days
34.4 Walk-Forward
The 0DTE Trading Rules paper uses strict out-of-sample conditional rules and rolling/expanding windows, with 252 trading days as a common burn-in/window length.
Use:
Training window = 252 trading days
Validation = walk-forward
Test = out-of-sample
No parameter tuning on test

36. Main Mistakes to Avoid
Mistake 1: Treating OI as Direction
Wrong:
High Put OI = bullish
High Call OI = bearish
Correct:
High OI = important level
Direction depends on dealer/customer side, gamma, IV, flow, and price confirmation
Mistake 2: Ignoring OI Timing
OI is often yesterday’s settled data. Intraday volume is today’s live activity.
Mistake 3: Treating SD as a Hard Wall
Part 4 warns about fat tails and gap risk.
Mistake 4: Selling Naked Options
Gold and 0DTE both punish short-gamma mistakes. The gold case study shows how short puts can rapidly create losses much larger than received premium.
Mistake 5: Ignoring Event Risk
0DTE jump risk is large, clustered around open/close, and strongly affected by macro events.
Mistake 6: Copying SPX Research Directly to Gold
Use SPX studies for mechanism, not exact thresholds.
Mistake 7: Ignoring Liquidity
The Trading Rules paper explicitly includes spread, depth, relative spread, and liquidity tightness because implementation frictions matter.

37. Final Portable Summary for Another Session
Copy this block into another session as the minimum instruction:
We are building an automated trading signal for futures, especially Gold/GC, using options data.

The system must follow the Mtraders philosophy: measurement, not prediction. Price shows what is happening; IV and OI show why it may be happening. OI is real but delayed and sign-ambiguous. OI is the map of committed positions, while intraday volume is the live activity. Do not use high Call OI or high Put OI alone as a buy/sell signal.

Core data:
- GC futures 1m OHLCV, VWAP, settlement, session high/low
- CME Gold options chain: strike, expiry, call/put, bid, ask, mid, volume, OI, IV, Delta, Gamma, Vega, Theta
- Prior settlement and Vol Settle / ATM IV
- Intraday volume by strike
- Event calendar
- Liquidity: spreads, depth, relative spread

Build static daily map:
- Anchor = prior settlement or CME settlement reference
- Vol Settle / ATM IV
- 1SD, 2SD, 3SD expected move bands
- Prior OI by strike
- OI-based gamma exposure by strike

Build dynamic intraday map:
- Current price
- Current IV / Vol Chg
- Intraday volume
- Volume/OI ratio
- Flow pressure
- Price location versus SD and OI/GEX walls

Formulas:
- Expected Move = Price × IV × sqrt(T)
- OI Notional = OI × Multiplier × Price
- OI Delta Exposure = OI × Delta × Multiplier × Price
- OI Gamma Exposure = OI × Gamma × Multiplier × Price²
- For Gold, multiplier = 100 troy ounces; 1 GC point = $100.

Classify regimes:
- Positive gamma: market makers hedge against price movement; mean reversion/pinning more likely.
- Negative gamma: market makers hedge with price movement; momentum/squeeze more likely.
- Unknown gamma: reduce size and require stronger confirmation.

Use rules:
- Mean reversion only when price is stretched near ±2SD or beyond, near OI/GEX wall, IV flat/falling, positive gamma or pinning behavior, and breakout fails.
- Momentum only when price breaks OI/GEX wall, volume/OI surges, IV expands, price holds the break, and gamma/flow regime supports continuation.
- No trade inside ±1SD unless strong flow confirms.
- No normal trade during major event windows.
- Never treat SD as hard wall.
- Never sell naked gamma.

Backtest:
- No lookahead on OI.
- Include spread, slippage, fees.
- Separate event and non-event days.
- Use walk-forward / out-of-sample testing.
- Use risk engine before entry engine.
