### A. CME / OI Wall Logic

#### 1. What does the guru mean?

The guru interprets Open Interest (OI) concentrations at particular strike prices from CME option data as areas where the financial obligations of option sellers (Market Makers/institutions) are densest. These clusters create structural boundaries or attractors in the market due to the systematic need for these institutional sellers to continuously manage risk via delta hedging (*Gamma Hedging*) to keep their portfolios dynamically neutral (*Delta Neutral*).

#### 2. What data is needed?

* **CME Option Open Interest (OI):** Specifically broken down by individual strikes, distinguishing between Call OI and Put OI.
* **Intraday Volume Change (Intraday OI / Vol):** Real-time contract accumulation throughout the day to detect where shifting participant interest is altering overnight positioning.
* **Implied Volatility (IV) & Implied Range:** To determine whether a wall falls within the boundaries of statistical pricing definitions.

#### 3. What condition makes the wall a target or magnet?

A wall acts as a structural magnet under two key conditions:

1. **Proximity (Within the 100-tick Boundary):** The guru states that a wall is only mathematically relevant as an active target if it is within a $\$100$ range of the current underlying price. Beyond $\$100$, institutional delta obligations decay sharply, neutralizing their gravitational effect on price.
2. **Gamma Squeeze Dynamics:** When the underlying price begins approaching a high OI strike accompanied by rising volatility, market makers are forced to aggressively buy or sell underlying futures contracts in the direction of the move to maintain neutrality, structurally dragging the price directly into the wall.

#### 4. What condition makes the wall a rejection zone (Resistance/Support)?

A wall functions as an authoritative rejection zone under two conditions:

1. **Low Market Volatility (Standard/Low Implied Volatility Days):** When Implied Volatility sits at standard or compressed levels (e.g., 1.5% daily expected move or lower), the underlying asset lacks the directional velocity required to clear institutional hedging walls. The wall is treated as a hard boundary.
2. **Confluence with Implied Volatility Boundaries (2SD/3SD):** If a high-volume overnight OI wall closely aligns with the pre-market calculated 2nd or 3rd Standard Deviation boundary, it transitions into a highly reliable mean-reversion boundary.

#### 5. What condition makes the wall break or acceptance valid?

A wall break or structural acceptance is validated when price breaks cleanly past the strike and holds above or below it on a sustainable timeframe (e.g., 1-hour candle closes), combined with expanding intraday volume. The guru emphasizes waiting for confirmation rather than front-running a breakout, noting that clean acceptance signals a cascade through structural liquidity voids.

#### 6. Does a put wall behave differently from a call wall?

The underlying risk architecture dictates their behavior, but both create structural boundaries. On an operational level, high concentrations of Put OI below price act as hard floors where market makers defend inventory or absorb spot market distribution via long-hedging adjustments. Call walls above act as institutional ceiling boundaries where short-gamma risk forces defensive actions. However, the exact directional impact is tied directly to whether positions represent institutional accumulation or distribution.

#### 7. Wall usage for Entry, TP, SL, or No-Trade

* **Entry:** Implemented as a mean-reversion counter-trend trigger when price pushes into a high-OI wall at extreme volatility boundaries (2SD to 3SD).
* **Take Profit (TP):** Consistently mapped directly to the nearest major structural OI wall or its exact midpoint within a designated grid block.
* **Stop Loss (SL):** Never placed precisely on a wall; it is systematically set at the half-block midpoint ($12.50$) or full-block boundary past the wall where institutional invalidation is absolute.
* **No-Trade:** Active when price is pinned in the middle of a high-volume wall cluster where mathematical edge drops to 50/50.

### B. SD / Range / Grid Logic

#### 1. Description and Calculation of SD/Grid

The guru maps the market using a structured framework combining mathematical volatility distributions with fixed structural increments:

* **The Baseline Equation:** The daily Implied Volatility parameter is systematically parsed using an institutional benchmark rule to derive a standard daily expected move:

$$\text{Daily Expected Move (\%)} = \frac{\text{Implied Volatility (IV)}}{16}$$



The denominator $16$ represents the approximate square root of trading days in a year ($\sqrt{256}$). This percentage is multiplied by the pre-market session opening price to determine the exact boundary of 1 Standard Deviation (1SD), capturing roughly 68% of expected historical distribution.
* **The Structural Grid:** This dynamic volatility calculation is then overlaid across a rigid, highly localized block matrix structured in structured pricing steps:
* **Full Block:** Structured explicitly as a **$\$25.00$** structural unit.
* **Half Block:** Set explicitly as a **$\$12.50$** structural unit.



#### 2. The $\$25$ Grid: Empirical or Fixed?

The $\$25$ increment functions as a structural grid unit derived from how CME options are specified and listed. It does not dynamically shift with daily price activity. Instead, it represents the standardized strike interval framework used by institutional participants to commit major blocks of capital. The guru aligns daily volatility calculations with these structural blocks to identify precisely which levels will trigger market maker hedging activity.

#### 3. Execution Thresholds at 1SD, 2SD, and 3SD

* **1SD Boundary:** Categorized strictly as a **No-Trade Zone** for directional execution or mean reversion. The guru repeatedly outlines that executing trades inside 1SD offers zero statistical edge, describing it as a pure coin-flip context.
* **2SD Boundary:** Defined as the initial baseline territory for executing counter-trend or mean-reversion trades.
* **3SD Boundary:** Marked as the premium execution zone. Pushing into 3SD is flagged as a rare, highly profitable opportunity where price has extended to statistical exhaustion.

#### 4. The Critical Importance of 3SD

3SD represents an extreme statistical deviation from the mean, capturing less than 1% of standard distribution under normal volatility assumptions. When price penetrates the 3SD boundary, it indicates that current market extensions have fully exhausted the options market's expected daily tail risk, presenting a premium entry point for institutional-grade mean-reversion fades.

#### 5. Use of 3.5SD for Stops and Invalidation

The 3.5SD level serves as an absolute structural stop because moves extending past 3.5SD represent a complete failure of the statistical models and option-chain parameters calculated during pre-market planning. A break beyond 3.5SD indicates an anomalous, high-velocity trend day (often driven by fundamental geopolitical or macroeconomic shifts) that invalidates the standard mean-reverting architecture.

#### 6. Setup Invalidation and TP Targets

* **Confirmation of Entry:** Requires price to pierce an outer volatility boundary (2SD/3SD) or test a primary structural strike block, followed by an immediate structural rejection.
* **Setup Invalidation:** Occurs if the underlying price closes completely outside the designated risk boundary or continues extending down to the half-block threshold beyond the structural level.
* **TP Placement:** Placed systematically at the **Midpoint** of the active structural block ($\$12.50$ away from entry) or at the opposing major block level ($\$25.00$ away).
